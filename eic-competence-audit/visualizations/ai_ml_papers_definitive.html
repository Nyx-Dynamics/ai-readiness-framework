What the Numbers Reveal

The Multiplier Effect: "AI" Triples "ML"

The broad search (263) is 3.1× the narrow search (86). But the multiplier varies dramatically by journal. NEJM, JAMA, and Annals show 5×+ multipliers — meaning most of their AI/ML content uses the term "artificial intelligence" rather than "machine learning." These are more likely editorials, commentaries, and policy pieces about AI rather than original ML research. Circulation, JID, and OFID show lower multipliers (1.3–2.3×), suggesting their content skews toward actual applied ML methodology.

NEJM: Declining, Not Growing

NEJM broad: 19→7→5. That's a decline. The world's most prestigious medical journal is publishing fewer AI/ML papers each year even as PubMed-wide AI/ML grows 67%. The NEJM AI silo strategy appears to be actively draining computational content from the flagship. In the narrow search, NEJM hit zero in 2025.

JAMA: The Volume Leader, Driven by AI Policy

JAMA leads all 9 journals with 80 broad-search papers over 3 years, growing 20→25→35. But its 5.7× multiplier (highest of any journal) signals that much of this is "artificial intelligence" commentary, not "machine learning" research. The November 2025 "JAMA Summit on AI" and the "Call for Submissions" editorial confirm JAMA is leaning into AI as a topic of discussion — Bibbins-Domingo is engaging editorially. Whether that translates to publishing more original ML research remains to be seen.

EID: 2 Papers in the Broad Search. Total.

Emerging Infectious Diseases — the CDC's flagship — published 2 AI/ML papers total in 2023–2025 using the broadest possible search terms. Zero in 2023. One in 2024. One in 2025. Meanwhile, genomic surveillance of SARS-CoV-2 variants, ML-driven antimicrobial resistance prediction, and AI-powered wastewater epidemiology were transforming the field EID covers. The journal's near-complete absence from the AI/ML literature is the single most striking finding in this analysis.

OFID: The ID Standout

Open Forum Infectious Diseases published 30 broad-search papers — more than CID (18), EID (2), and CMR (3) combined. Its trajectory (4→8→18) is the steepest growth curve of any ID journal. Its low multiplier (1.8×) means most content is actual ML methodology, not AI commentary. OFID's open-access model and lower impact factor may paradoxically make it more receptive to computational work that higher-IF journals gatekeep more aggressively.

The 0.115% Bottleneck

These 9 journals — the most influential editorial platforms in US general medicine and infectious diseases — published 0.115% of all AI/ML papers indexed in PubMed over 3 years. The other 99.885% appeared in informatics journals, engineering journals, specialty computational journals, and preprint servers that most practicing clinicians never read. This creates a translation bottleneck: the science exists, but it doesn't reach the clinicians who need it through the channels they trust.

ID Is Growing Fastest — From the Lowest Base

ID journals grew from 10 to 42 broad-search papers per year (+320%), vs. Gen Med's 54 to 76 (+41%). But ID started from almost nothing. Even at 42 papers in 2025, all 5 ID journals combined published fewer AI/ML papers than JAMA alone (35). The gap between where ID needs to be computationally and where its publication infrastructure is remains enormous.
