Fourteen EICs across the highest-impact US-based medical journals — general medicine and infectious diseases — were reviewed. Not a single one has formal training in machine learning, computer science, biomedical informatics, or data science. Not a single one has published a first-author paper using ML methods. What This Means

🔴 0 for 14: A Perfect Shutout

Across 14 US-based journal EICs — spanning the most influential general medical and infectious disease publications — there is not a single editor with formal ML training (no CS degree, no informatics certificate, no ML coursework) and not a single first-author publication applying machine learning methods. The score is 0-for-14. This is not a sampling artifact; these are the people who control the publication pipeline for virtually all high-impact US medical research.

🟡 The "Editorial Engagement" Tier: Policy, Not Practice

Three editors engage with AI at the editorial policy level: Rubin (launched NEJM AI, delegated to Kohane), Bibbins-Domingo (convened JAMA Summit on AI, appointed Rohan Khera as AI associate editor, published AI guidance for authors), and Sax (CID publishes ML-informed ID studies and Sax has engaged editorially with AI topics). But editorial engagement means writing policy about AI, not doing AI. None of these three has trained a model, written a line of Python, or evaluated an ROC curve from their own ML pipeline.

🟣 The "Adjacent Methods" Exception: Bibbins-Domingo

Bibbins-Domingo comes closest — she has a PhD in epidemiology, chaired UCSF's Dept. of Epidemiology and Biostatistics, and has used simulation modeling (Markov models, microsimulation) in cardiovascular disease research. These are sophisticated quantitative methods. But simulation modeling is not machine learning. The mathematical foundations, validation approaches, and interpretive frameworks are fundamentally different. Calling simulation modeling "ML-adjacent" is generous.

🧬 The PhD Paradox: Right Rigor, Wrong Domain

Six of 14 EICs hold PhDs: Rubin (microbiology), Bibbins-Domingo (epidemiology), Hill (molecular biology), McNally (cardiovascular genetics), Casadevall (microbial pathogenesis), Sears (implicitly through research focus). These are rigorous doctoral programs. But none teaches gradient descent, cross-validation, neural network architecture, feature engineering, or any core ML curriculum. The EICs have advanced quantitative training — in the wrong quantitative domain.

🏥 The ID-Specific Gap Is Total

Among the 6 US-based ID journal EICs (Sax, Fiore, Sears, Bedimo, Patel, Casadevall), the ML credential count is: zero training, zero publications. Meanwhile, ML is actively transforming infectious diseases through: genomic variant surveillance (SARS-CoV-2, influenza), AMR prediction from whole-genome sequencing, sepsis early-warning systems using EHR data, drug-target interaction modeling for antimicrobial development, and epidemic forecasting. The editors deciding which of these studies reach top journals have never built a model themselves.

⚖️ The Structural Workaround vs. The Competency Gap

Journals are aware of the gap and are patching it: NEJM created a whole new journal (NEJM AI) and hired Kohane. JAMA appointed Rohan Khera (Yale, deep learning researcher) as AI associate editor. CID's editorial board includes methodologists. But these are deputy-level patches. The primary gatekeepers — the people who make the initial editorial decision on whether a submitted ML study gets sent for external review, and who select which reviewers evaluate it — have no personal ML competency. They are entirely dependent on their deputies and external reviewers for technical assessment of ML methodology.

📊 Institutional Distribution: Harvard Still Leads, But ID Is Broader

Harvard holds 3 of 14 EIC seats (Rubin/NEJM, Inouye/JAMA IM, Sax/CID) — 21% of US editorial leadership from a single institution. Johns Hopkins adds 2 (Sears/JID, Casadevall/mBio). Together, Harvard + Hopkins = 5/14 (36%). The remaining 9 seats span UCSF, Thomas Jefferson, UT Southwestern (×2), Northwestern, UVA, UW, Mayo Clinic, and CDC. ID journals show more institutional diversity than general medicine: Mayo, CDC, VA, and UT Southwestern are all represented.

💡 The Paradox Worth Publishing

Machine learning is the fastest-growing methodological approach in biomedical research. The number of PubMed-indexed papers mentioning "machine learning" grew from ~2,000/year in 2015 to >30,000/year in 2024. These papers are submitted to journals whose editorial gatekeepers have zero hands-on ML experience. This creates an asymmetry: the editors rely entirely on external reviewers for technical ML assessment, but they select those reviewers — a task that itself requires ML competency to do well. This is not an accusation of incompetence; it is a structural observation about how the editorial system has not kept pace with methodological evolution.

Scope & Exclusions: This analysis covers 14 US-based EICs across 8 general medicine/specialty journals and 6 infectious diseases journals. Excluded: all non-US journals (Lancet family, BMJ, CMI, IJID), professional editors without US academic appointments, and Isaac Kohane (NEJM AI) as a purpose-built outlier. JAMA Cardiology excluded because Barbara Casadei (incoming 2026 EIC) is UK-based at Imperial College London.

Methodology: EIC CVs reviewed via institutional faculty profiles, Google Scholar, PubMed, ResearchGate, journal "About the Editors" pages, Wikipedia, and IDSA/ASM/AHA announcements. "First-author ML publication" = paper where EIC was first or corresponding author using ML/DL/AI methods (not merely mentioning AI in an editorial). "ML training" = any degree, certificate, or documented coursework in computer science, data science, biomedical informatics, or machine learning. Impact factors from JCR 2024 (Clarivate 2025); approximate (~) where exact data behind paywall.

Caveat: The absence of personal ML credentials does not necessarily mean poor editorial decision-making about ML papers. Editorial judgment involves clinical relevance, study design, public health impact, and ethical considerations beyond technical ML methodology. However, the complete absence of any ML practitioner experience among 14 primary editorial gatekeepers is a structural observation that warrants attention as ML becomes central to biomedical research methodology.
