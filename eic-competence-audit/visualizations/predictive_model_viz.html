Can They Judge What They Can't Do?

A predictive model for the probability that an EIC correctly triages an AI/ML manuscript, given zero formal ML training across all 14 editors

Bayesian triage model · 9 journals · 5 paper types · Narrow + Broad · Feb 2026
The Model

Core Question

When an AI/ML manuscript arrives at an EIC's desk, the editor makes three sequential decisions. Each requires different levels of ML expertise. The probability of correct triage is the product of getting all three right.

P(Correct Triage) = P(Desk Decision) × P(Reviewer Selection) × P(Adjudication)

Step 1: Desk Decision

Reject or Review?
Can the EIC recognize if the ML methods are plausible enough to send for external review?

ML expertise needed: Moderate. Must distinguish legitimate ML methodology from methodological nonsense.
→

Step 2: Reviewer Selection

Who Reviews?
Can the EIC identify reviewers with the specific ML subdomain expertise to evaluate this paper?

ML expertise needed: High. Must know the difference between a CNN expert and an NLP expert, or a random forest user and a transformer specialist.
→

Step 3: Adjudication

Accept or Reject?
When reviewers disagree on ML methodology, can the EIC weigh competing technical claims?

ML expertise needed: Highest. Must evaluate arguments about overfitting, data leakage, architecture choices, validation schemes.
Model 1: Narrow — Personal ML Competence

Can the EIC personally evaluate ML methodology? This is the "desert island" model — no deputies, no board, just the editor and the paper. Score 0–1 with a hard ceiling of 0.25 without formal ML training.

Why Reviewer Selection Is the Kill Step

An EIC evaluating an ML paper about transformer-based AMR prediction from whole-genome sequencing needs to know: Is this a job for a bioinformatician? A deep learning specialist? Someone who works specifically with sequence models? An AMR microbiologist who uses random forests? Each would evaluate the paper differently, and only the right match catches the right flaws. An EIC without ML training cannot distinguish these subspecialties — they see "someone who does AI" as a single category. This is equivalent to asking a cardiologist to choose between a rheumatologist and a nephrologist for a lupus consult.

The Deputy Fix: Necessary but Insufficient

NEJM (Kohane) and JAMA (Khera) have appointed ML-credentialed deputies. This boosts their P(Correct) substantially — Bibbins-Domingo's triage probability for novel ML papers is 0.475 vs. 0.142 for Fiore (a 3.3× difference). But even with a deputy, P(Correct) never exceeds 0.50 for methods-heavy papers. The deputy isn't always consulted, doesn't always see the paper first, and cannot fully substitute for an EIC's own technical judgment during adjudication.

The ID Journal Desert

All five ID journal EICs score between 0.142 and 0.156 for novel ML methods papers. None has an AI deputy. None has a quantitative PhD. Sax's editorial engagement (CID) gives him a marginal edge (0.156 vs. 0.142), but the practical difference is negligible. For methods-heavy ML papers submitted to ID journals, the model predicts a ~85% error rate at the EIC triage level.

ML Training Helps Most Where It's Needed Most

The counterfactual gain scales with paper complexity: +0.042 for editorials, +0.166 for novel methods. But even with ML training, P(Correct) for novel methods only reaches 0.391 — still a 61% error rate. This means ML training is necessary but not sufficient. The editorial triage system itself needs structural reform: dual ML+clinical review tracks, computational methodology checklists (like TRIPOD+AI), and mandatory ML-specialist desk review before any editorial decision.

Model Assumptions & Limitations

Explicit Assumptions

1. Multiplicative independence: The three triage steps are modeled as independent — P(Correct) = product of three probabilities. In reality, a bad desk decision can cascade (sending a flawed paper to review wastes reviewer time and may still lead to publication if reviewers miss the same flaw the EIC missed).

2. Binary ML training: ML competence is modeled as present/absent. In reality, there's a spectrum — someone who completed a Coursera ML course is different from someone with a PhD in statistical learning theory. The model's ceiling at 0.25 for untrained editors is a judgment call based on the observation that domain transfer from traditional statistics to ML is incomplete.

3. Deputy substitutability: An AI deputy is modeled as providing 35% of the boost of personal ML training across triage steps. The actual substitutability depends on how early the deputy sees the paper, their authority to overrule, and how much the EIC defers to their judgment.

4. Publication volume as proxy: A journal's ML publication volume is used as a proxy for editorial board ML capacity. This assumes that journals publishing more ML papers have developed better infrastructure for evaluating them — a reasonable but imperfect assumption.

5. Base rates: Even without ML training, experienced editors correctly desk-reject obviously flawed papers at a base rate of ~30% for ML methodology (based on general pattern recognition) and ~90% for clinical content. These base rates are estimated, not empirically measured.

What This Model Does NOT Capture

Selection bias: ML researchers may self-select away from journals where they expect poor evaluation, creating a feedback loop where the journals that need ML papers most receive the fewest.

Type II errors: The model focuses on triage accuracy (can the editor correctly prioritize?), not on the direction of errors. A false desk-reject of a transformative ML paper is a much larger loss to science than a false acceptance of a mediocre one.

Reviewer quality: Even correctly selected reviewers may not be available, may decline, or may themselves have limited ML expertise in the specific subdomain. The model assumes a correctly selected reviewer provides accurate assessment.

Scope: 9 US-based journals (4 general medicine, 5 infectious diseases). 14 EICs total (some journals share JAMA Network EIC infrastructure but have distinct specialty EICs). Kohane excluded as purpose-built outlier. Non-US journals excluded.

Interpretation: P(Correct Triage) represents the probability that the editorial triage process reaches the same decision that would be reached by an ML-competent editor evaluating the same paper. It does not imply that current decisions are always wrong — some correct triage occurs through clinical judgment, reviewer selection luck, and editorial board support. The model quantifies the structural disadvantage, not the actual error rate.
