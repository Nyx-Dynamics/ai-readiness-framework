What This Reveals

EID: Zero. Not One.

Emerging Infectious Diseases — published by the CDC, impact factor 6.6, 6th-ranked ID journal — has published zero papers with "machine learning" in the title or abstract since 2020. The last ones were in 2019 and 2020. This is a journal covering emerging pathogens in an era where ML-driven genomic surveillance tracked every SARS-CoV-2 variant in real time. The new EIC (Fiore) has no ML training.

NEJM: 6 Papers in 3 Years — Then Zero in 2025

The world's most prestigious medical journal published 5 ML papers in 2023, 1 in 2024, and 0 in 2025. This isn't because ML stopped being relevant to medicine. This is a journal that launched NEJM AI specifically to siphon ML content away from the flagship — effectively creating a separate ghetto for computational research rather than integrating it into the main publication.

OFID and JID: The ID Bright Spots

Open Forum Infectious Diseases (17 papers) and Journal of Infectious Diseases (15 papers) published more ML papers than CID (7), EID (0), and CMR (1) combined. OFID's open-access model may lower barriers for computational researchers. Both show upward trajectories (OFID: 3→5→9; JID: 1→6→8). These are the ID journals most likely to be receptive to ML work.

Circulation: The Quiet Leader

Circulation leads all 9 journals with 19 ML papers over 3 years, steady at 6-7 per year. Cardiology has embraced ML earlier than most specialties (ECG interpretation, imaging, risk prediction). Yet its EIC (Joseph Hill, molecular cardiologist) has zero ML credentials — the board does the heavy lifting.

The Paradox in Numbers

These 9 journals — representing the most influential editorial gatekeepers in US medicine and infectious diseases — account for 0.062% of all ML papers indexed in PubMed. Meanwhile, their editors shape which ML research gets the imprimatur of clinical authority. The 139,120 ML papers that didn't land in these 9 journals still exist. They're in specialized informatics journals, engineering journals, and preprint servers — where clinicians almost never read them. The funnel is absurdly narrow, and the gatekeepers have no training in evaluating what passes through it.

The Conservative Search

These counts use "machine learning" only in title/abstract. Adding "deep learning," "artificial intelligence," "neural network," and "natural language processing" would increase the numbers — but even doubling or tripling the count wouldn't change the fundamental finding. Whether it's 86 or 250 papers across 9 journals over 3 years, the scale relative to 139,206 PubMed-wide remains vanishingly small.
